<p>Muslims found an unlikely opening to voice their opposition to mainstream stereotypes, exploiting a social media misstep by <em>Newsweek</em>&#8216;s inflammatory cover on Muslim violence. In response to <em>Newsweek</em>&#8216;s call to discuss the problem of violent muslim intolerance through the Twitter hashtag, #muslimrage, clever users flooded the discussion with <a target="_blank" href="http://www.wired.com/threatlevel/2012/09/muslimrage/?utm_source=feedburner&amp;utm_medium=feed&amp;utm_campaign=Feed%3A+wired%2Findex+%28Wired%3A+Top+Stories%29">(hilarious) parodies</a>, like <a target="_blank" href="https://twitter.com/LSal92/statuses/247763296541896705">this gem</a>: &#8220;Lost your kid Jihad at the airport. Can&#8217;t yell for him. #MuslimRage.&#8221; The now viral parody has punctured the mainstream wall of negative press to give an otherwise silent majority a (seriously) funny voice.</p>
<p>After widespread violent protests throughout the Arab world sparked by an offensive YouTube video of the prophet Muhammad, op-eds began pouring out of the nation&#8217;s pre-eminent media outlets questioning whether Islam was compatible with democratic tolerance. Op-eds ranged from the transparently hawkish Fox News (&#8220;<a target="_blank" href="http://www.foxnews.com/opinion/2012/09/13/mrs-clinton-and-mr-obama-it-time-to-wake-up-and-smell-global-jihad/">It&#8217;s Time To Wake Up And Smell The Global Jihad</a>&#8220;) to the historically nuanced <em>Washington Post</em> (&#8220;Why Is The Arab World So Easily Offended?&#8221;).</p>
<p><em>Newsweek</em> attempted to capitalize on the discussion with an over-the-top all-caps cover, &#8220;MUSLIM RAGE&#8221;, but the overwhelming hilarity of the tweets broke <em>Newsweek</em>&#8216;s grip on their own conversation.</p>
